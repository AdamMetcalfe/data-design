Chapter 21

Common Visualization Mistakes

Welcome to the last but certainly not least chapter of the book!  We hope you’ve learned enough to appreciate how much good data and good design can help you communicate your message.  Professional data visualizers get excited by the stories they want to tell as well, but sometimes they forget to follow some best practices while doing so.  It happens to the best of us.  So in this chapter, we’re going to cover what those best practices are.

 

Pro Tip #1: Don’t truncate your axes.

One of the ways a graph can be distorted is by truncating an axis.  This happens when an axis is shortened because one or both of its ends gets cut off.

Sometimes a distortion like this is really obvious.  For example, let’s say there are two allergy medicines called Happajoy and Pollaway.  The bar graph below compares how effective these two medicines are at reducing the tearful, congested misery known as allergy symptoms.  If you quickly glance at the bars, you may think that Happajoy is twice as effective as Pollaway is because its bar is twice as tall.  But if you examine the graph more closely, you’ll see that the y-axis is truncated, starting from 30.2 and going up to only 30.7 percent.  The truncated y-axis makes the difference between the two bars look artificially high.  In reality, Happajoy’s effectiveness is only 0.2% higher than Pollaway’s, which is not as impressive as the results implied by the bar graph. 

 Sometimes a truncated axis and the resulting distortion can be more subtle.  For example, the next graph shows the quantity of Happajoy sold from January through May 2014. 


As you can see, the y-axis is truncated again, starting from 5 million and going up to only 5.5 million. But the critical thing to understand here is that the x-axis has also been truncated - we’re seeing sales from less than half the year.  Truncating a time period like this can give the wrong impression, especially for things that go through cycles.  And - you guessed it - the sale of allergy medicine goes through a seasonal cycle since allergy symptoms are typically higher in the spring and lower in the winter.

What would be a better way to show sales of Happajoy?  Something like the graph below:

This graph shows the same data set of Happajoy sales, except this time the y-axis is proportional and the x-axis covers two full years instead of just a few months.  We can clearly see that sales of Happajoy went down in the winter and up in the spring, but that the rate of sales didn’t change much from year to year.  In fact, sales were a little lower in spring 2014 than in spring 2013.

When you compare the last two graphs of Happajoy sales, do you see how different their stories are?  If you were an investor in Happajoy’s company and you saw the graph with truncated axes, you might dance happily through the streets because it seems like the company is doing really well.  On the other hand, if you saw the graph with proportional axes, you might reach for some aspirin instead of Happajoy because of the headache you’d get worrying about the overall decrease in sales. 

So watch out for truncated axes. Sometimes these distortions are done on purpose to mislead readers, but other times they’re the unintentional consequence of not knowing how truncated axes can skew data. 

 

Pro Tip #2: Don’t leave out key variables.

The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool.

 - Richard Feynman, 1974 Caltech Graduation Address

You know how famous people are sometimes criticized for something they said, and they often reply that they were quoted out of context?  Context is important, especially when it comes to data.  It’s very easy to fool yourself by leaving out variables that could affect how you interpret the data.  So whenever you’re examining a variable and its relationships, carefully consider the ecosystem in which that variable exists and deliberately seek out other variables that could affect the one you’re studying. 

This is easier said than done.  For example, the map below shows each state’s market leader in allergy medicine: Happajoy is the leader in dark blue states, while Pollaway is the leader in light blue states.  On the surface, it might seem like Happajoy is the market leader nationally, ahead of Pollaway.  But to get the complete picture you have to pay attention to other variables.

For example, the bar graph below shows a breakdown of market share in each state.  (We’re only going to look at the western continental states for now.)  The margins by which Happajoy leads are significantly less than the margins by which Pollaway leads. 

Combine the information from the bar graph with the table below.  The total sales in states where Happajoy is the leader is also significantly less than the total sales in states where Pollaway is the leader.  When you add up the numbers, Pollaway’s total sales are more than twice that of Happajoy’s.  When all of this additional information is taken into account, is it still accurate to say that Happajoy is the national market leader in allergy medicine?

Pro Tip #3: Don’t oversimplify.

Life is complicated, right? Data can be complicated, too.  Complicated is hard to communicate, so it’s only natural to want to simplify what your data are saying.  But there is such a thing as simplifying too much.  Oversimplifying is related to the previous point about not expanding the scope enough to provide a clear picture of what’s going on.

For example, let’s say you’re an investor of RediMedico, the maker of Happajoy, and you attend the annual sales meeting.  The CEO of RediMedico starts off the presentation with the following graphic:

Now the investor in you might look at that graphic and start daydreaming about all the wonderful things you’ll do with such a great return on your investment.  But then the data pro in you kicks in, you snap back to reality, and you start wondering about what that 14% increase means.  You ask yourself:

  • Compared to what
  • Compared to when?
  • Compared to whom?
  • How have these numbers changed across time?

These are all worthwhile questions to answer with a visualization!  Thankfully, the CEO of RediMedico agrees and presents the next graphic, which compares the revenues from the five top-selling medicines RediMedico makes:

If we do some number-crunching, we see that the average increase in revenue between 2013 and 2014 is indeed 14%. However, we also see that this increase is primarily due to a whopping 225% increase in revenue from a single medicine, Exoalgio. Revenue from 3 out of 5 medicines actually dropped.  So RediMedico’s first graphic tells part of the truth, while the second graphic tells the whole truth by presenting the details behind the single number. 

Using a graphic with a single number and no breakdowns is like writing a news headline without the news story. Keep the headline - that revenue improved by 14% - and then provide the context and the background to flesh out the full story. 

Remember that when communicating with data, a single number is not meaningful on its own. It’s better to make the numbers meaningful by breaking them down and comparing them. Try to be true to the underlying complexity by digging deeper into the data and providing readers with a better understanding of the numbers you’re presenting.

 

Pro Tip #4: Don’t choose the wrong form.

Creating a data visualization is a balancing act between form and function.  Alberto Cairo, a well-known expert in the field, wrote a book called The Functional Art - a title that nicely captures the dual roles of data visualization.

When choosing a graphic format for your data, you’ll have to figure out how to effectively communicate to your audience in an aesthetically pleasing way.  This may seem like a daunting task, but fear not!  There’s actually a lot of research that can help us with this balancing act.  In fact, you’ve already been introduced to some of this research in the previous chapter - Cleveland and McGill’s “Graphical Perception” paper, in which they rank how well different graphic forms help people make accurate estimates. 

You can use this scale to help you choose the best graphic form for your data. Area and shading are good at giving readers a general overview that helps them understand the big picture. Graphs with a common baseline (such as bar graphs) are good at helping readers make accurate comparisons.

Since we’ve already looked at examples of bar graphs and line graphs in this chapter, let’s take a look at a couple graphics that use area and shading.

The first graphic uses area to display the units of Happajoy sold in each state. Based on bubble size, you can generally tell that more Happajoy was sold in California than in Texas.  But can you tell by how much?  Is the California bubble eight times bigger than the Texas bubble?  Ten times?  It’s hard to tell.  It’s even harder to tell when the bubble sizes are closer together: Did New York or Washington sell more Happajoy?  We run into the same problem with the second graphic, which uses shading to represent Happajoy sales: California is definitely darker than Texas, but how much darker? Eight times? Ten times?  Who knows?  This is why area and shading are better for giving an overall picture instead of making precise comparisons.

In addition to area and shading, angles also give us a tough time when it comes to making accurate estimates.  This is why it’s so hard to compare two pie charts, as in the example below.

It’s already hard to precisely compare the slices within the *same* pie chart.  It’s even harder to compare slices across different pie charts. If the goal of this graphic is to help readers compare Happajoy sales in each state from one year to the next, then something like a bar chart would have been a better choice.

That’s the key thing: Think about which graphic forms will best facilitate the tasks you want your readers to do.

 

Pro Tip #5: Do encode data in multiple ways.

There may be situations where you should encode your data more than once in order to give readers a more accurate picture of what’s going on. For example, Figure 13 is a graph from the New York Times in 2009 entitled “How Different Groups Spend Their Day.” As you can see,  the y-axis is a 100% scale and the x-axis is a time scale. The graph shows several categories of how Americans are spending their day: eating, work, household activities, traveling, TV and movies, and socializing. You can explore the graph online here.

What is this graphic form useful for? It is very good at giving you the “big picture” of the data and to show that all the variables add up to 100%, which you can clearly see on the y-axis.  But what if you want to see how one of these categories is changing across the day? It is really difficult to do. For example, try to estimate how “household activities” are changing throughout the day. It actually seems to be decreasing just because the baseline of that particular portion of the graphic is going down. The other variables around it are actually pushing its baseline continuously. As a result, it’s not very easy to estimate how much “household activities” are changing during the day. 

So what did the designers do? They encoded the same data more than once. If you click on any of the categories, all the other categories flatten so you can see how how the category you clicked changes through the day. Figure 13a only shows how much time people spend on “household activities” over the course of the day. So in this example, multiple graphic forms encoding the same data let readers do or see different things. This is something I believe many visualization designers forget.

Another example of this is a graphic I made with my team in Brazil showing the evolution of the world’s population. It begins with a line graph shown in Figure 14, but when you click on “Mapa” it displays changes in world population through the years with a slider that enables you to see changes over time, as in Figure 14a. There are tons of bubbles here! And remember what bubbles are for? They are useful for giving you the big picture, the overview, but nothing else. 

With just bubbles, how can you possibly compare one country to another country, or see the ranking of countries or how much your own country has changed through the years? But those are all tasks your reader may want to complete, so we decided to encode the data more than once! If you click on “ranking” you will get a ranking of all the countries in the world. If you go the map and click on any of the bubbles, you will get a line graph so you can see how your own country has changed through the years:

If you are interested in how your country compares to other countries you can open the drop-down menu, click on any other country and get a comparison between the United States and any other country in the world. It’s exactly the same data, just encoded in a different graphic format.

Multiple graphic forms allow you to see different things in the data, a critical task. Encoding the data only once is a mistake that I believe many visualization designers make!

Before you begin, always consider ways to encode the same data in multiple formats so its easy for readers to see different things.

 

Pro Tip #6:  Do add text to annotate.

They say that a picture is worth a thousand words, but that doesn’t mean you should forget about words entirely!  Even your most beautiful and elegant visualizations can use text to highlight or explain things to your audience.  This is especially useful when you’re presenting multiple layers of information because your annotations can help readers connect the various pieces into an understandable whole.  And obviously, you’re not reading this book in order to make boring visualizations, right?  Good visualizations engage an audience, so adding text is a great way to address questions that may come up as curious readers examine your graphic.

For example, let’s take a look at the following pollen forecast map:

You can see that there is generally more pollen in the middle and eastern parts of the country, but what are the stories that are hidden in the data and are unexplained? For example, why does that one particular spot in the center of Texas have such a low pollen count compared to the rest of the South?  Curious readers would like to click over here and get an explanation of some kind.

An example of a beautifully annotated visualization is an infographic by The New York Times about breast cancer. The designers organized the information as a narrative with a step-by-step structure. After clicking “Begin”, you’re presented with a scatterplot:

The scatterplot shows that there is an inverse correlation between breast cancer detection and mortality. In other words, as more women are detected with breast cancer, fewer women die from it. But notice how the designers use text to highlight another aspect of this scatterplot - that highly developed countries have higher diagnosis rates (and lower mortality rates) while the opposite is true for the developing world.

As you keep clicking “Next”, the designers guide you deeper into the scatterplot by highlighting a cluster of countries and providing an annotation that gives you further insight into that cluster.  If you’re curious about the statistics for a specific country, you can mouse over that country’s dot to get those numbers.

Now imagine this graphic with no annotations, no headline introduction, no nothing. We would be left with a relatively straightforward scatterplot that leaves little room for us to explore the data further.  By providing useful and well-placed annotations, the designers help us see relationships that we otherwise may have missed. 

So whenever you create a visualization, think about the “So what?” of your graphic: Why should people care about the information you’re presenting?  Add annotations to help them understand why they should care.  Write a good headline that catches their attention, a good introduction that highlights interesting data points, and a good narrative that structures your visualization logically. Good writing is an important part of good visualizations.
