Chapter 20

Ways to Lie with Graphs

âWith great power comes great responsibility.â

Although we could argue who should be credited with this phrase, we can all undoubtedly relate to it or any number of its variations. In the case of this book, we have given you incredible power and tools to use in building compelling stories through visualization. How that power is used is ultimately up to you, but be aware: misusing this power could unintentionally mislead audiences.

In the following sections, we will explore ways that the brain operates and naturally desires to seek out discrepancies and define boundaries in our visual field. We will look at mechanisms that can fool our brains at a high-level as well as ways this can happen at a more granular scale.

Contrast

Our brains love a difference. They immediately seek to discriminate between background and foreground and to define boundaries of objects (e.g., where your coffee cup ends and the table begins). The detection of these boundaries is based on variations of light intensity and color; therefore, the higher the contrast between two adjacent objects, the more likely they are identified to be differentCairo, Alberto. The Functional Art. Peachpit Press: 2012..

Color

Since the difference in colors is a primary way that contrast is established, it is imperative that we use color to ensure we do not deceive the brain. In designing graphics, we can use color to focus the mind on a particular, desired object or on whole areas of our graphic.

For example, if we wanted to emphasize (or de-emphasize) a particular trend within a multi-line graph, all we have to do is adjust the shading. As shown in the example below, the trend in red is more prominent and detracts from the rest of the series where anomalies in the data can hide.

If our objective is to highlight a particular section of an infographic, we can adjust its background shading to stand out above other, less relevant sections. To our eyes, reds and yellows come to the foreground, while blues recede to the backgroundRockwell, Ken. The Secret: What Makes a Great Photo. 2013: http://www.kenrockwell.com/tech/basics.htm. Accessed 1 March 2014.. As a result, components shaded in blue could seem less relevant, while the eye is focused on more prominent components shaded in red.

Similarly, we can use shading to group data elements that would otherwise appear discrete.

Shapes

Shapes are also incredibly important in helping the brain differentiate entities, although they work the opposite way that colors do. Our brains have a much harder time discerning shapes than they do colors. Designers often think that using pictograms—simplified, pictorial symbols for words, phrases, or objects—is a useful or playful way to illustrate an idea or to make an attractive infographic.

What happens, however, is that the designer is forced into a trade-off between too much and too little detail. If a designer wants to compare similarities or differences between schools and churches on a map they might want to add a lot of detail to the pictograms so that they are distinguishable. Unfortunately, the more details included, the busier a graphic becomes. If the designer takes the opposite approach, their pictograms may become indistinguishable, as shown below. This can dupe the brain into mis-reading the information.

Our brains are notoriously bad at discerning values encoded in area graphs, particularly those seen as bubble charts or circle graphs. Designers have started to use bubble charts in their infographics to represent their data. Alignment

Even illustrations that use the appropriate types of graphs can still be misleading if they don't use appropriate alignment. Without appropriate alignment in our illustrations, graphs can become void of the reference points we use to make comparisons. The Details: Units, Labels, Axes, and Scales

As we work our way down the list of visual cues, we find that graphs can easily lie to the reader when particular details—units, labels, axes, and scales—are cluttered or inconsistent. By mixing minute details, designers can introduce inconsistencies across visualizations, which can fool readers into misunderstanding the data. The graph below illustrates how these details can mislead the reader.
