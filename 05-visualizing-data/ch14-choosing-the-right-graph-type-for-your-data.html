Choosing a graph type

Once you've figured out which and how much data you want to visualize, you need to figure out what type of graph you want to use to tell your story.  There are only certain graph types that will make sense for your data and, of those, some will help illustrate your point better than other.

There are a few important questions to ask yourself when you're trying to decide what type of graph to use.

How many variable are you trying to graph?

Some types of charts and graphs lend themselves to simplicity and work best when you're only working with one or two variables.  Pie charts, basic bar graphs, and histograms fall into this group.  If you have multiple variables that you are trying to show, you will usually do better with a more complex graph type, such as a line graph or a stacked bar graph.  (This assumes, of course, that the variables you are attempting to visualize together make sense to have in a single graphic.  Keep in mind that more complex is not always better, and sometimes the best choice is to decrease the amount of information that you are trying to display in one place.)

Type of variables

Each kind of graph only works with specific data types.  For instance, you use pie charts to show what percent of the total group falls into each of several categories, so it only makes sense to use them with categorical or discrete data. Overall purpose of graph--what story about your data are you trying to tell?

Once you've figured out what types of graphs make sense for your data, you need to figure out which one type will work best for your data.  In order to do this, you have to identify what story it is about your data that you're trying to tell.  

Are you interested in a single timepoint or multiple timepoints?  If you're just focusing on a single, cross-sectional moment in time, you may do fine with a more basic visualization like a single pie chart. However, if you're looking to show numbers across time, you'll either need to show several pie charts or something with an axis that allows you to display the change in a variable over time. {single timepoint graphs and multiple timepoint graphs}

Similarly, if the main focus of your narrative is on trends, you may find it beneficial to choose a graph type that allows you to easily visualize the progression of a trait over time.  {graph of cartogram over time}  Interactive visualizations may be of particular benefit here when they can be utilized. If your main focus is on comparisons between groups, side-by-side visualizations such as comparative pie charts, bar charts, or overlaid line graphs allow an audience to immediately see similarities and differences between your categories of interest.  These types of visualizations are common and can be extremely beneficial in helping illustrate a narrative, but be sure not to mislead your audience with inappropriate use of layout or color (see page XXX).

Finally, if your interest is in exploring overall patterns in your data set or the distribution of your data, there are specific types of graphs that will allow you to do just that.  Scatterplots let you see where every individual point in the data set lies so you can get a sense of the spread of the data.  Histograms tell you what the frequency distribution of the data is for a given variable so you can see range and variance.

Pie Charts

Data types: discrete; can use continuous data, but need to break into groups first

Used for: Showing what percent of the total group or a particular subgroup fall into each of several specificed categories

Pros: Quick representation of distribution of the group; can be very useful if several pie chart are shown side by side as a means of comparison

Cons: No external reference point when a singular pie chart; don't have any sense of what a single distribution means out of context

Vertical Bar Graphs

Data types: X-axis: discrete; Y-axis: discrete or continuous

Used for: Showing the value of a discrete or continuous variable for several different groups

Pros: Allows you to make quick visual comparison between groups; clean graph

Cons: If groups are nominal, ordering is arbitary, so you can change the impact of graph just by reordering the categories; if bars are not labeled, can be hard to determine exact value that each bar represents

Horizontal Bar Graphs

Data types: Y-axis: discrete; X-axis: discrete or continuous

Used for: Showing the value of a discrete or continuous variable for several different groups

Pros: Allows you to make quick visual comparison between groups; clean graph

Cons: If groups are nominal, ordering is arbitary, so you can change the impact of graph just by reordering the categories; if bars are not labeled, can be hard to determine exact value that each bar represents

Stacked Vertical bar graphs (Note for Trina: The clarity of this one will depend highly on the visuals so make sure to make these clear)

Data types: X-axis: discrete; Y-axis: discrete or continuous

Used for: Showing the values of a discrete or continuous variable for several different groups within several different groups

Pros: Allows you to make quick visual comparison of the distribution of the subgroup values between groups

Cons: Same as regular bar graphs; also, difficult to compare actual values of the subgroups within each main group

Histograms

Data types: X-axis: an ordinal or continuous variable broken into groups; Y-axis: counts of how many observations in the data set have a value that falls into that group

Used for: Showing the frequency distribution of a data set for an ordered or continuous variable

Pros: Allows you to quickly gauge the overall distribution of the variable in the data set

Cons: Depends on how fine the categories are; if too small or too big, the shape of the distribution will seem off

Line Graphs

Data types: X-axis: continuous; Y-axis: continuous

Used for: Showing how the value of one continuous variable changes as the other continuous variable changes; can be grouped by a third variable

Pros: Allows you to see trends and correlations; can quickly compare groups if lines are overlaid

Cons: Important to remember that correlation is not causation - graph can be misleading if not properly interpreted

Scatterplots

Data types: X-axis: continuous; Y-axis: continuous

Used for: Showing all the data pairs of X and Y in a data set; can be grouped by a third variable

Pros: Allows you to see overall spread of data and cluster effects

Cons: Like line graphs, important to note that correlation is not causation; if patterns are noticed, can be reason to research further, but the graph alone is not proof of association

Cartograms

Data types: geographic variable and one or more discrete or continuous variables

Used for: Showing the value of one or more discrete or continuous variable on their corresponding map locations

Pros: Gives an immediate view of the geographic distribution of a trait; general meaning is usually readily understood since the graph is map-based

Cons: Can be complicated to correctly interpret if the distinctions between variable levels or geographic locations are not clear enough
