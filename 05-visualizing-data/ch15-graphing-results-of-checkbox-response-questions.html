Chapter 15

Graphing the Results of Checkbox Responses

Checkbox responses

This chapter focuses on checkbox responses or multiple response questions, where a question can be answered with more than one answer, if applicable.

Refresher on the difference between checkboxes and radio buttons

Let's say you're doing a survey and you're interested in what multimedia devices your respondents have used over the last 6 months. You would use a checkbox response question if you wanted to find out what all the devices were that people used over the six-month period. A radio button only allows respondents to select a single answer, so you could only use it to find out, for example, which one device a person used most often during the six-month period. Each type of question has merit, it just depends on what the purpose of your question is and how you are going to use the results.

How checkbox answers are received

In the example below, we asked, “Which of the following electronic devices have you used in the past 6 months? Please select all that apply.” The premise behind the question is that it’s likely that a respondent could use more than one electronic device over a 6-month period, such as a cell phone and a tablet.

How your results or raw data are compiled will, of course, depend on the program you are using to program your survey. One of the more common formats is what you would see in an Excel spreadsheet.

How results of checkbox questions are special

Let's take a look at a summary of possible responses to the checkbox question posed above.

Table 1 Electronic Devices Used Total
PC 421 (84%)
Mac (desktop) 300 (60%)
Tablet (any kind) 285 (57%)
iPad 185 (37%)
Laptop 200 (40%)
Cell phone (any kind) 450 (90%)

You may notice that the total number of responses (1,841) is greater than the number of people that did the survey (N=500)! Why? Well, when you ask a checkbox question, you're not really asking just one thing; you're asking a series of yes/no questions. A respondent checks a box if an answer choice applies and leaves it blank if it doesn't. The total possible number of checked boxes? (# of "real" answer options) X (# of respondents) (Here, a "real" answer option means one that isn't "None," "N/A" or "Prefer not to Answer," since selecting one of those options would prevent a person from choosing any other answers in additional to that.)

There are a few different ways to illustrate these findings, depending on what your overall question of interest is. One way is to do a bar graph and base the percentages on the number of respondents that selected each answer choice, like in the graph below. Clearly, cellphones (90%) and PCs (84%) are the most commonly-cited electronic devices used in the past six months.

However, the fact that the total adds to more than 100% can be unsettling for some. An alternative way to illustrate the results is to base the percentages on the total mentions (1,841).

A drawback to this way of displaying data is one must always keep in mind that it is based on mentions not people, so one can’t say, for example, ‘24% of consumers used a cellphone in the past six months’. {we need a better example here of when this could be an advantage, a case where mentions might be okay to use and not just confusing}

Attitudinal Measurements

So far, we’ve been looking at the use of checkbox questions to gather data on basic counts (e.g. electronic devices used). Checkbox responses can also be used to assess agreement with attitudinal statements. The graph below shows attitudes of homeowners towards owning their home.
