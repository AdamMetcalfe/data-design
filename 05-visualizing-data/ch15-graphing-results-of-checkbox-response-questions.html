Graphing the Results of Checkbox Responses

Checkbox responses

This chapter focuses on checkbox responses or multiple response questions, where a question can be answered with more than one answer, if applicable. In the example below, we asked, “Which of the following electronic devices have you used in the past 6 months? Please select all that apply.” The premises behind the question is that it’s likely that a respondent could use more than one electronic device over a 6-month period, such as a cell phone and a tablet.

There are a couple of ways to illustrate these findings.

One way is to show the data as reported (Graph 1A below). Clearly, cellphones (90%) and PCs (84%) are the most commonly-cited electronic devices used in the past six months (Note: These results are purely fictional). Another thing to note is that the order of the devices has been changed so they are in descending order, beginning with the largest. This is important in communicating the results in an easy-to-understand manner.

But the fact that the total adds to more than 100% can be unsettling for some. An alternative way to illustrate the results is to base the percentages on the total mentions (1,841).

A drawback to this way of displaying data is one must always keep in mind that it is based on mentions not people, so one can’t say, for example, ‘24% of consumers used a cellphone in the past six months’.

Comparison of Checkbox Responses and Single-Choice Questions

So, you can use a checkbox response question if you want to find, in our example, all the devices one could use over a six-month period. In contrast, a single-choice question would see to find out which one device a person uses most often in a six-month period. Each type of question has merit, it just depends on what you want to find out (what are your objectives) and how you are going to use the results. The graphs below compare results from a checkbox response question and a single-choice question.

Another difference between single choice and checkbox responses is that in the former type of question, if you want to change your answer you just select another answer and your first answer is automatically de-selected. For checkbox responses, you must select your incorrect answer again in order to de-select it.

Other Ways of Using Checkbox Responses

So far, we’ve been looking at simple measurement attributes (e.g. electronic devices used). Checkbox responses can also be based on attitudinal statements. Writing checkbox response questions

Here are a few tips for writing this type of question:

  • Randomize answer options to help prevent order bias

    Order bias are survey bias by which responses can be affected by the order of answer choices. Respondents tend to favour responses at the beginning and at the end of a list because they are more memorized. First items may also be chosen by respondents to save time.

    The Digital Marketing Glossary

  • If there is any change that a respondent won’t be able to answer the question (“Can’t say” or “don’t know”), might not want to answer the question (“prefer not to say”) or might have an answer different from any of the answers provided (“other”) ut this option at the bottom of the list and anchor it or make sure it always appears as the last option.
  • If you have a “none” option, make sure it is exclusive, that is, if it is selected, all other options are automatically de-selected.

Order of mentions

When programing your question, you may want to consider which device was selected first, second, third, etc. This information os often used in marketing to help determine which devices are “top of mind”.
