The subjective nature of data storytelling

Even when captured and analyzed with great care, data is always subjective. Analysts editorialize, whether they like it or not, because of the practicalities of using data for storytelling. These editorial decisions in data come from three places.

First: Deciding what to collect.

We have chosen to collect gender, name, and coffee type. We didnât collect data on glasses of water consumed, or shoe type, or eye color. This is ultimately an editorial decision, and itâs one reason that no data is ever entirely honest.

Can we fix it? As technology improves, we may overcome this limitationâfor example, a bar camera connected to machine learning that can extract dimensions of data from the raw footage years laterâbut for the foreseeable future, simply choosing what to record is subjective. Itâs a decision that is strongly influenced by our biases, including the stories we hope to tell and the arguments weâre hoping to justify.

Second: Deciding how to aggregate

When we roll up data into buckets, or transform it somehow, we take away the raw history. For example, when we turned raw transactions into annual totals:

  • We anonymized the data by removing the names of patrons when we aggregated it.
  • We bucketed timestamps, summarizing by year.

Either of these pieces of data could have shown us that someone was a heavy, habitual drinker (based on total alcohol consumed by one person, or based on the rate of consumption from timestamps.) Did we have a moral obligation to warn someone? What if the patron had killed someone while drunk? Is the data subject to legal discovery? Are we allowed to aggregate it?

Can we fix it? The big data movement is going to address some of this. Once, it was too computationally intensive to store all the raw transactions. We had to decide how to aggregate things at the moment of collection, and throw out the raw information. But advances in storage efficiency, parallel processing, and cloud computing are making on-the-fly aggregation of massive data sets a reality, which should overcome some amount of aggregation bias.

Third: Deciding how to communicate

Humans canât consume large amounts of raw data. When we tell stories, we select the narrative, and choose where to shine the spotlight. At the start of this, we said that 36.7% of Coffee shots in 2000 were consumed by women. Thatâs an editorial decision, which has broad impact.

Itâs easy to communicate a factoidâbut does it somehow bias people? What if 2000 were an exception, and in other years women consumed significantly more? Do I have an obligation to present it in context?

Can we fix it? This is one of the things that interactive, exploratory infographics fix. Because they partly hand the decision about how to communicate over to the audience, they encourage discovery and transparency. The key is to include the source information in the visualization, and to turn a display into an interactive interface that encourages users to click, segment, drill down, and query in new and unexpected waysâways that even the author of the source material didnât anticipate.
